Second Mission: Foreign Agent

After your incredible work on tracking extraterrestrial life you were promoted to oversee an entire division uncover foreign spies. Your team while talented needs your help.

You are working in SIGINT for an intelligence office in the federal government with top secret clearance. One of your field operatives intercepted and recorded a radio signal being transmitted by a known sleeper agent working for a foreign government, and has securely transmitted the recording to your office. You are tasked with reading the demodulated radio message to see what was transmitted.

You already know the sleeper agent is not acting very carefully and therefore isn’t protecting their message using encryption; there will be no need to decrypt this message using some sort of cipher. However, you have been informed the message intercepted is a digital transmission, and this is where you come in. You have only been provided with the raw waveform recorded by your field operative’s radio receiver. Therefore, you will need to demodulate this signal from a list of samples.

You have intel that indicates this sleeper agent has been using a digital transmitter that leverages Amplitude Phase Shift Keying (APSK) modulation (see: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude_and_phase- shift_keying).

The field operative has sent a text file to you for you to demodulate using code. This file is comprised of many samples of a single, full transmission that was intercepted. Individual lines of the file represent the signal intensity (amplitude) that the radio receiver read at that point in time, as human-readable floating point numbers. Every group of 32 lines in the file are a collection of samples (floating point numbers) that become one symbol of the APSK signal transmitted. Each symbol is a sine wave modulated in two manners: phase and amplitude. A reference “carrier wave” to which the transmitted phase is calculated against is a sine wave, which completes its full period over 32 samples in the text file.

An APSK symbol is comprised of two components:
1. The phase shift (offset) of the signal, the X axis 2. The amplitude of the signal, the Y axis

Phase can shift up to 50% of the waveform, so multiply the phase by two to get a value ranging from 0 (fully in phase) to 1 (180 degrees out of phase). Amplitude does not need any scaling and can be treated as linear for the purposes of plotting on the Y axis from 0 (silent) to 1 (full volume).
